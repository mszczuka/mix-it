Assistant — primer on VContainer & UniTask

VContainer

A dependency injection (DI) framework for Unity. Instead of every script calling FindObjectOfType<Wallet>() or manually wiring references in the Inspector, a single "composition root" registers services and Unity injects them into constructors/fields automatically. Why it matters for us: swapping the plugin (liquid puzzles → something else) becomes a one-line change in registration instead of hunting through prefabs. Alternatives: Zenject (older, heavier, more magic), plain Microsoft DI (painful in Unity), manual DI (fine for small projects). VContainer is the modern Unity-idiomatic pick: lightweight, fast, no reflection voodoo.

UniTask

A replacement for Task/async-await optimized for Unity. Unity's main thread + frame loop doesn't play nicely with .NET's Task (allocations, threadpool assumptions, no frame-aware awaits). UniTask gives you await NextFrame(), await DelaySeconds(1), zero-alloc awaits, and proper cancellation on scene unload. Industry standard in Unity 2020+. Alternative is coroutines — works but can't return values, hard to compose, no try/catch across yields. For IMatchSession.Run() returning a MatchResult, async is dramatically cleaner.

Both are free, open-source, well-maintained, and used across virtually every serious Unity studio. Going with VContainer + UniTask.

Assistant — Design proposal

Approaches considered

  1. Scaffolding-first — all boundaries, DI, contracts first; nothing playable for weeks.
  2. Vertical slice MVP — play loop in 2 weeks with loose boundaries, refactor later. But the "C" (full platform) choice makes this the wrong trap.
  3. Contract-first + vertical execution ← recommended. Pay boundary cost early where cheap (asmdefs, IPlugin, SO config skeleton, DI — all shells). Prove with a vertical slice (home → match → result → home). Then parallelize remaining screens against stable contract.
